**Chapter 1: The Calm Before the Storm**

The wind howled outside, rattling the windowpanes of Anna’s small apartment. It was one of those cold autumn evenings where the air felt heavy, as if the world itself was holding its breath. The street was quiet, save for the occasional rustle of leaves scraping across the pavement, carried by gusts that seemed to whisper secrets only the night could hear.

Inside, the soft glow of a single lamp flickered, casting long shadows across the room. Anna sat on the couch, cradling a mug of tea in her hands, trying to find some sense of calm. But nothing felt quite right. The tea was too strong, or maybe too weak—it didn’t matter. Everything was off tonight.

She sighed, placing the mug on the coffee table, her eyes drifting to the window. The familiar creaks of her apartment settling mixed with the hum of the refrigerator, usually comforting, now felt unsettling. Like the quiet was too quiet, the shadows too dark.

Anna shook her head, brushing off the creeping unease. It was just another October evening. She was being ridiculous.

But a small, irrational voice inside her whispered otherwise.

Suddenly, the lamp flickered, just for a moment, before steadying again. Anna froze, staring at it, waiting for the next flicker. When nothing happened, she let out a breath and tried to laugh at herself. It was just the wind. Maybe a loose connection.

Still, the unease lingered, a heavy sensation in the pit of her stomach.

She reached for her phone, hoping to distract herself. As she scrolled through social media, even that felt wrong. The cheerful updates from friends felt hollow, and the smiling faces in group photos seemed forced, distant. The more she scrolled, the more agitated she became, a low simmer of frustration building inside her.

Her stomach growled, reminding her she hadn’t eaten much for dinner. But the thought of getting up and cooking felt like too much effort. Everything felt like too much tonight. She knew it wasn’t hunger gnawing at her—something else was stirring, deep inside, unsettling and uncomfortable.

The wind outside picked up again, howling through the trees, making the apartment feel even more isolated. Anna glanced at the clock—late, but not too late. The evening stretched out before her like a yawning void. Time felt strange, slower, heavier, like it was dragging her into some inevitable conclusion she wasn’t ready for.

She rubbed her eyes, another sigh escaping her. It was ridiculous to feel like this. She was just tired, that’s all. Everyone got a little moody sometimes, right? But the irritation bubbled up inside her, faster than it should. Everything was wrong—the taste of the tea, the flickering light, the endless scrolling. It all grated on her nerves.

And then, like the click of a lock turning, it hit her. The calendar.

Her stomach sank. With a slow, sinking dread, Anna opened her phone and checked the date.

Exactly one week.

One week until her period.

She stared at the numbers on the screen, her breath catching for a moment. Of course. Of course, that’s why she felt so off. The irritability, the weird taste, the restless mood—it all made sense now. The shadow that had been following her all evening, the tension that wouldn’t leave—it was just the beginning.

The Week Before the Period.

The words echoed in her mind like a warning, and she could almost hear the universe itself letting out a low, ominous hum.

"Great," she muttered, tossing her phone back onto the couch. "Just great."

Another gust of wind rattled the windows, more forceful this time, sending the curtains fluttering. The light flickered again, casting jittery shadows across the walls. And for a brief moment, Anna could’ve sworn she saw something outside—a figure, standing just beyond the window, watching her.

Her heart skipped a beat, body freezing in place, eyes locked on the shadow. But a second look told her it was just a trick of the light, a tree branch swaying in the wind.

She exhaled, muscles slowly relaxing, though the feeling of being watched didn’t fully leave her.

"I hate this week," Anna muttered to herself, pushing herself off the couch. Her body felt heavy, the exhaustion of the day and the weight of what was coming pressing down on her. She knew what was coming. The bloating. The breakouts. The low energy that would make everything feel like a chore.

It was the same every month. A small, personal horror she couldn’t escape.

As she switched off the light and made her way to bed, the wind outside seemed to howl louder, as if mocking her.

* * *

**Chapter 2: The First Signs**

Anna woke to the sound of her alarm buzzing insistently from the nightstand, cutting through the thick fog of her sleep. She groaned, slapping her hand down on the phone to silence it. As she lay there, staring at the ceiling, she could already feel it—her body felt heavy, bloated, like she had swallowed a lead balloon overnight. Her head throbbed with the dull weight of sleep that hadn’t been quite restful.

“Ugh…” she muttered, rubbing her face. The Week Before. It was always like this.

She pulled herself up to sit on the edge of the bed, resting her elbows on her knees. Her body protested, every movement sluggish. Even the thought of getting up and starting her day felt like too much. Her stomach churned uneasily, and she placed a hand over it, feeling the familiar bloat pushing against her waistband.

Anna glanced over at her phone, the clock reminding her that time was ticking away. Another sigh. She couldn’t stay in bed any longer—she had to get to the office today. Her hybrid schedule meant this wasn’t an option she could skip, even if her body screamed at her to retreat under the covers.

She forced herself to her feet and padded over to the mirror, dreading what she’d see. Sure enough, there it was: a small but angry red bump on her chin, the first of what would likely be several. A sigh of resignation escaped her lips. “Great. Just great.”

It was like clockwork—acne, bloating, fatigue. Her body betraying her in the lead-up to every period, as if reminding her of its control. She turned her face to the side, checking for more breakouts, running her fingers over her skin, and grimaced. Her skin felt oily and uneven. Even her hair seemed limp and lifeless today, hanging down like it had lost its will to cooperate.

She pushed herself through the morning routine—brushing her teeth, washing her face, pulling her hair back into a messy bun. It was harder than usual to summon the energy to care. Every little thing felt exaggerated, heavier. Her toothbrush scraped her gums, the water was too cold, the towel too rough. Each irritation stung more sharply than it should have.

And then there was the matter of finding something to wear.

Anna stood in front of her closet, staring at the rows of neatly hung blouses, pants, and skirts. Normally, she had no trouble choosing an outfit for the office, but today the thought of anything structured made her skin crawl. Everything looked uncomfortable. Too tight, too formal, too much.

She pulled out a pair of loose black pants and an oversized sweater, throwing them onto the bed. It wasn’t exactly office chic, but at this point, she didn’t care. Comfort trumped everything today. She wasn’t in the mood to impress anyone.

As she dressed, she caught sight of herself in the mirror again. The clothes sagged on her in a way that only made her feel more bloated, more unattractive. She turned away quickly, trying not to linger on the sight.

The morning sunlight filtered in through the thin curtains, casting a pale glow over the room. It should have felt peaceful, but instead, it only reminded her how much time had passed. She was running late. Again.

Her stomach gave a low rumble, but the thought of breakfast turned her off. She couldn’t tell if she was actually hungry or just unsettled. Either way, she wasn’t in the mood for food. She grabbed a banana and her keys, forcing herself out the door.

* * *

The hallway outside her apartment was quiet, almost eerily so. Anna descended the stairs, each step heavier than the last, the dull weight of fatigue settling into her limbs. By the time she reached the front door, it felt like she had already run a marathon.

The crisp autumn air hit her as she stepped outside, a slight chill running through her. Normally, she loved autumn—the fresh air, the changing leaves, the sense of transition. But today, even that felt off. The wind tugged at her sweater, the dry leaves crunching underfoot grated on her ears. It was like the whole world had turned just slightly against her.

As she walked to her car, Anna’s thoughts began to spiral. The small annoyances of the morning seemed to snowball. The waistband of her pants was digging into her bloated stomach, the sweater she thought would be comfortable now felt scratchy against her skin. Even the way the wind blew her hair into her face made her want to scream.

She slid into the driver’s seat, slamming the door shut with more force than she intended. The sound echoed in the enclosed space of the car, making her wince. As she sat there for a moment, hands gripping the steering wheel, she felt the familiar wave of frustration begin to rise.

The Week Before. It always started like this. The small things, the little annoyances that built up, one after the other, until they became too much to handle.

Anna took a deep breath, trying to steady herself. It’s just a day, she reminded herself. You can get through it. Just get to the office, do your work, and go home.

But as she started the car, the gnawing feeling in her gut told her it wouldn’t be that simple.

* * *

**Chapter 3: Fractures in the Mask**

The drive to the office was uneventful, but that didn’t stop Anna from feeling the strain of every traffic light, every slow car that seemed to deliberately block her path. By the time she pulled into the office parking lot, she was already gripping the steering wheel a little too hard.

“Just breathe,” she whispered to herself, exhaling slowly. She had made it. Half the battle was just showing up, right?

She grabbed her bag and trudged into the building, hoping that maybe today would be quieter than usual. She could sit at her desk, put on her headphones, and avoid unnecessary conversations. She hoped for peace, but deep down, she knew that wasn’t how today would go.

As soon as she stepped into the open-plan office, she was greeted by the low hum of chatter, keyboard clacking, and the buzzing of phones. Normally, the sounds blended into white noise, something easy to tune out. But today, every single noise felt like it was pressing against her skull.

She walked toward her desk, doing her best to avoid eye contact. But before she even had a chance to sit down, it began.

“Morning, Anna!” came the overly cheerful voice of Emma from two desks over. “How was your weekend?”

Anna forced a smile, but it felt like pulling teeth. “Fine, thanks. Yours?”

“Oh, it was great! We went to this amazing little winery just outside the city, you should totally check it out sometime. The wine was sooo good.”

“Sounds nice,” Anna said, hoping the short response would end the conversation. But of course, Emma wasn’t done.

“And the weather was perfect! You know, that crisp fall air, but with the sun still shining? It was gorgeous. You’ve got to go.”

Anna nodded stiffly. Inside, she was seething. She had no energy for small talk, no patience for Emma’s endless enthusiasm. It was like nails on a chalkboard, every word grating on her already frazzled nerves.

She finally managed to sit down, putting her bag on the floor and booting up her computer. But the noise around her continued—someone was typing too loudly, someone else was having an animated phone call nearby, and somewhere in the distance, the sound of a printer whirring into action made her jaw tighten.

She clenched her fists under her desk. Why did everything have to be so loud today?

Anna pulled on her headphones, hoping that some music would drown out the chaos, but even the familiar tunes seemed irritating. The volume felt off—either too loud or too quiet—and no matter how much she fiddled with it, she couldn’t get it right. Everything was wrong. Again.

And then, just as she was about to focus on her inbox, an email notification popped up. It was from Greg, her team lead, asking her to join a quick meeting later in the afternoon. Normally, she would have shrugged it off—just another part of the job—but today, the mere thought of sitting in a meeting room with people asking her questions made her stomach turn.

Quick meeting, he’d said. There was no such thing as a quick meeting with Greg.

Anna closed her eyes, trying to center herself. She could feel the frustration rising, the tension knotting up her shoulders. It wasn’t fair—none of it. She just wanted to be left alone, to get through the day without having to fake smiles or engage in pointless chit-chat.

But that wasn’t how the office worked. The open-plan space felt suffocating, every person in her proximity an unwelcome presence. She could hear snippets of conversations around her—people discussing weekend plans, complaining about the weather, debating lunch choices. Things she usually brushed off now felt like personal attacks on her ability to focus.

And there it was, that familiar knot in her chest, tightening. It was happening again.

A coworker from accounting, Dan, walked past her desk, loudly crunching on an apple. The sound was unbearable. The wet, smacking noise of the fruit being chewed made her physically cringe. It was like each bite sent a jolt of irritation straight through her.

“Oh my God, please,” she whispered under her breath, gripping the edge of her desk.

Dan didn’t even notice as he wandered over to another desk, chatting casually with someone else, his apple crunching away. Anna’s pulse quickened, the tension building in her throat. How could something so small, so stupid, drive her so mad?

Her heart thumped in her chest, her skin prickling with the overwhelming urge to escape. Everything was too much—the sounds, the people, the clutter of the office. Even the air felt thick and oppressive, like it was weighing her down.

Anna stood up abruptly, her chair scraping against the floor. A few people looked up, but she didn’t care. She needed out. Now.

Without a word, she headed straight for the restroom, her steps quick and purposeful. As soon as she pushed through the door, she locked herself in a stall and leaned against the wall, closing her eyes and willing herself to breathe.

In the small, dimly lit space, the noise of the office was muffled. It wasn’t silence, but it was something close. She could hear her own breathing, shaky and uneven, as she tried to calm the storm raging inside her.

Her hands trembled as she pulled out her phone. She had an app for tracking her cycle, and without thinking, she opened it, checking again.

Six days until her period.

Six more days of this.

Anna leaned her head back against the wall, closing her eyes again. It didn’t matter how many times she went through this—every month, it still hit her like a freight train. The irritability, the fatigue, the frustration at things that didn’t deserve it. All of it building until it felt like she might explode.

In the quiet of the restroom, she finally allowed herself a moment to feel it all—the anger, the exhaustion, the overwhelming desire to just hide from the world.

It was only the beginning, and already, she wasn’t sure how she was going to make it through the day.

* * *

**Chapter 4: Breaking Point**

Anna stood in front of the mirror in the restroom, splashing cold water on her face. The chill helped—just a little. She gripped the sides of the sink, looking at herself in the harsh fluorescent light. Her reflection stared back at her, pale, tired, and tense. The dull throb behind her eyes hadn’t gone away, and her stomach still felt bloated and uncomfortable.

“Just get through the day,” she muttered to herself.

With one last deep breath, she straightened up, adjusted her sweater, and walked out of the restroom, trying to shake off the tension clinging to her like a second skin.

* * *

Back at her desk, the familiar blue glow of her computer screen seemed almost too bright, making her squint as she sat down. Anna put her headphones back on, trying to tune out the world once more. But even the comforting hum of music couldn’t drown out the constant buzz of the office around her.

She opened her inbox. Fifty unread emails.

“Fantastic,” she muttered, her fingers already hesitating over the keyboard.

The emails themselves were nothing out of the ordinary—questions about projects, status updates, requests for meetings. But every new message felt like a weight pressing down on her. Normally, she could handle this with ease. Today, it felt insurmountable.

Just as she was beginning to respond to an email, her instant messenger pinged.

Emma: “Hey, Anna! Can you help me with this presentation real quick?”

Anna clenched her jaw, taking a deep breath before typing a reply.

Anna: “Sure. Send it over.”

Seconds later, the file popped up in her inbox. She clicked it open, scanning through the slides. It was fine. Fine enough, anyway. A couple of grammar tweaks and formatting issues, nothing major. But she knew that Emma wasn’t really asking for feedback—she just wanted an excuse to talk.

Sure enough, Emma appeared at the edge of her desk a moment later, leaning against the partition with that same chipper smile. “Thanks so much, Anna! You’re a lifesaver.”

Anna bit back a sigh. “No problem.”

“So, did you have a chance to check out that winery I mentioned? Seriously, it was sooo good. You’d love it.” Emma’s voice was light and bubbly, completely oblivious to the rising irritation in Anna’s expression.

“No, I haven’t,” Anna said, her voice clipped. She didn’t want to be rude, but every word Emma spoke felt like sandpaper against her nerves.

“Oh, you really should! Maybe this weekend, right? It’s the perfect time of year for it, with the fall colors and all. You’d—”

“Emma,” Anna interrupted, a little sharper than she intended. “I’m kinda busy right now.”

Emma blinked, clearly surprised by the tone. “Oh, sure! Sorry to bother you.” She gave a quick wave and disappeared back to her desk, her energy undimmed despite the brush-off.

Anna rubbed her temples, the headache pounding louder in her skull. She felt bad immediately after snapping at Emma—she hadn’t meant to. But she was tired. Tired of pretending to be interested, tired of holding it all in.

The frustration was building, each little annoyance feeding into a growing storm inside her.

* * *

An hour passed, but it felt like an eternity. The emails kept coming, and her focus wavered. No matter how hard she tried to concentrate, her mind kept drifting back to her discomfort—her bloated stomach, the tension in her shoulders, the way her sweater felt scratchy against her skin.

And then there was Dan.

Dan wasn’t a bad guy. He was friendly enough, always offering a smile or a joke as he made his rounds through the office. But today, his presence was grating. He lingered by her desk, cracking jokes about the printer jamming again, or complaining about the latest memo from HR. Usually, she could nod along, maybe even chuckle politely. But today, she couldn’t even muster that.

She kept her head down, hoping he’d get the hint and leave. But Dan, oblivious as always, leaned in a little closer. “You doing alright, Anna? You look kinda tired.”

Her hands froze on the keyboard.

What was it about people commenting on how you looked that made it so much worse? She knew she looked tired—she felt like she’d been hit by a truck. But hearing someone else say it out loud, especially when she was already struggling to keep it together, felt like a slap in the face.

“I’m fine, Dan,” she said, her voice tight.

“You sure? You know, if you need to take a break or something—”

“I said I’m fine,” Anna snapped, her voice rising louder than she intended.

Dan blinked, taken aback by the sudden outburst. “Whoa, okay. Sorry. Didn’t mean to—”

“I just… I need to focus right now, okay?” She didn’t mean to sound so harsh, but the words came out before she could stop them.

Dan raised his hands in mock surrender. “Alright, alright. I’ll leave you to it.”

As he walked away, Anna slumped back in her chair, guilt already washing over her. She shouldn’t have snapped like that. Dan didn’t deserve it, and neither did Emma earlier. But she couldn’t help it. Every small thing was building up, pushing her closer and closer to the edge.

The buzzing of the office continued around her, but Anna barely heard it. All she could think about was how everything was spiraling out of her control. The irritation, the anger—it was growing stronger, threatening to overwhelm her.

She glanced at the clock. Still hours left in the workday.

How was she going to survive this?
